Triangular Dreams envisions an experimental communication hall in Korea, where movement becomes experience. Pedestrian flow is guided upwards through a sequence of platforms, culminating in an ascent through a monumental oculus. Geometry leads the way: an infinite prism composed of equilateral triangles, forming an organic structural system that evokes the branching game of nature.

At the heart of the proposal lies a parametric script driven by a set of variables — dimensions, angles, number of communication pathways — and a randomisation algorithm. The resulting geometry was not sculpted manually, but emerged from a flexible yet controlled logic, allowing complexity to unfold within a framework of clarity.

The design also embraces modularity, with a repeatable building unit, making the overall geometry eventually feasible in real-world application — where form, function and feeling converge in a vertical journey towards the sky.

The Algorithm

Infinite Generation

The core of the system was an infinite generation algorithm, where each press of a button produced a new iteration of the design. Despite the randomness, the process remained fully controlled — boundaries were defined for each key variable, and design outputs were linked to an analysis tool that evaluated performance, generating a pool of iterations to learn from.

Each iteration was evaluated against structural and spatial performance criteria, building a dataset of possibilities from which the most promising configurations could be identified and refined — design as a process of discovery.

Left: structure detail — equilateral triangles as the repeatable building unit · Right: parallel coordinate analysis of design iterations across structural and spatial performance metrics
Structural Logic

Modularity and Buildability

Despite its organic appearance, the geometry is rooted in a single repeatable building unit: an equilateral triangle. This modularity not only makes the structure legible from the inside — where the triangulated pattern creates a striking visual field — but also ensures the overall geometry remains feasible for real-world construction, with standardised connections and a manageable number of unique elements.

The infinite generation algorithm demonstrated that controlled randomness and structural discipline are not opposites — but partners in a design process that produces genuinely novel results from a clearly defined set of rules.
